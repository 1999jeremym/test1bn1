Key Terms (from the glossary)

Anatomy — the study of the structure of the body and the relationships between body parts. For example, identifying where the heart sits in the thoracic cavity.

Physiology — the study of the functions of body parts and how they work. For example, understanding how the heart pumps blood. Anatomy and physiology are always studied together because structure determines function.

Respiration — the process by which cells produce energy (ATP) from oxygen and glucose, releasing carbon dioxide as a waste product. Note: this refers to cellular respiration, not just breathing (which is called ventilation).

Atom — the smallest unit of a chemical element that retains the properties of that element. Atoms make up molecules, which make up the chemical level of body organisation — the very foundation of the levels of organisation you studied.

Sebaceous — relating to sebum, an oily/waxy substance. Sebaceous glands are found in the skin and secrete sebum into hair follicles to lubricate and waterproof the skin and hair (part of the integumentary system).

 Describe what determines if something is living or non-living

Characteristics of All Living Organisms>

A common mnemonic to remember these is MRSGREN — Movement, Respiration, Sensitivity, Growth, Reproduction, Excretion, Nutrition.

1. Movement Living things move — either the whole organism (e.g. walking, swimming) or internally (e.g. blood moving through vessels, gut contracting). Even plants move slowly toward light.

2. Respiration Living things produce energy (ATP) from nutrients at the cellular level. This is cellular respiration, not just breathing. Every living cell carries out some form of respiration to fuel its activities.

3. Sensitivity / Irritability Living things detect and respond to changes in their environment (stimuli). For example, pulling your hand away from heat, pupils dilating in dim light, or a plant turning toward sunlight.

4. Growth Living things increase in size or complexity over time by producing more cells or increasing cell size. Growth is organised and controlled — not just random accumulation of material.

5. Reproduction Living things produce offspring, either sexually or asexually. This is essential for the continuation of a species. Note — an individual organism can survive without reproducing, but life as a whole cannot.

6. Excretion Living things remove the waste products of their own metabolic processes. For example, the lungs excrete CO₂, the kidneys excrete urea. This is different from simply eliminating undigested food.

7. Nutrition Living things take in and use nutrients to fuel metabolism, growth, and repair. This could be through eating, photosynthesis, or absorbing nutrients.

 Describe the major functions of the 4 types of tissues

Functions of the 4 types of tissues

What are the functions of epithelial tissue?

Protection, absorption (gut), filtration (kidneys), secretion (hormones, enzymes, mucus), and excretion of waste.

What are the functions of connective tissue?

Binding structures together (tendons, ligaments), support and framework (bone, cartilage), protection and cushioning (fat, bone), insulation, and transport (blood).

What are the functional characteristics of all muscle tissue?

Excitability (responds to stimuli), contractility (can shorten), extensibility (can stretch), elasticity (springs back to resting length).

What are the functional characteristics of nervous tissue

Detects stimuli and transmits electrical impulses around the body. Made up of neurons (conduct impulses) and neuroglia (support, protect, and nourish neurons).

 Explain how the structure of specific tissue helps the tissue with its function (e.g.
How does the dense irregular connective tissue of the dermis help the dermis to
do its job?)

Structure of specific tissues and how it helps with function

Structure → function in tissues

The rule: Every feature of a tissue exists because of the job it does.

1. Dermis — dense irregular connective tissue Collagen fibres run in all directions within an extracellular matrix. Fibroblasts produce and maintain these fibres. Because fibres go every way, skin resists tearing from any direction.

2. Alveoli — simple squamous epithelium One layer of flat cells with flattened nuclei, minimal cytoplasm, on a basement membrane. Wall is as thin as possible so O₂ and CO₂ diffuse across quickly.

3. Skin/mouth/oesophagus — stratified squamous epithelium Many layers of flat cells. Outer cells are dead and packed with keratin. New cells made at the basement membrane push upward to replace worn-away ones. Barrier never breaks down.

4. Heart — cardiac muscle Striated cells (organised actin and myosin = forceful contraction). One nucleus per cell. Connected by intercalated discs — gap junctions pass electrical signals instantly, desmosomes hold cells together. Whole heart contracts as one unit.

5. Airways — pseudostratified ciliated columnar epithelium Tall cells (need space for cilia machinery) all sitting on a basement membrane. Goblet cells produce mucus to trap pathogens. Cilia sweep mucus upward and out of the lungs.

6. Gut/bladder/blood vessels — smooth muscle Spindle-shaped, one nucleus, no striations. Contracts slowly, never fatigues, can shorten greatly. Perfect for hollow organs that need to move or change size automatically for hours.

 Describe special characteristics of epithelial tissue (e.g. Avascular)

Special characteristics of epithelial tissue

Special characteristics — every type of epithelium has ALL of these

Avascular — no blood vessels. Gets nutrients by diffusion from tissue underneath. Basement membrane — anchors the epithelium to the tissue below. Tightly packed cells — no gaps, forms a continuous sheet. Polarity — one side faces outward (apical), one side faces downward (basal). Innervated — has nerve supply. Regenerates fast — constantly replacing worn-out cells. Forms boundaries — always sits between inside and outside of the body.

Epithelial tissue

 Describe the function and locations of connective tissues (e.g. Adipose, hyaline

How to Identify Connective Tissue

Connective tissue

How to identify it — connective tissue always has:

  • An extracellular matrix — non-living material surrounding the cells
  • Fibres in that matrix (collagen, elastic, or reticular)
  • Scattered cells — not tightly packed
  • Found internally — never on a body surface
  • Variable vascularity — some types highly vascular, some none at all
Function and locations of connective tissues

Connective tissue — types, locations, functions

Areolar (loose CT) 

Where: under skin, around organs and blood vessels.

What it does: fills gaps between structures and holds organs in place.

Adipose (fat) 

Where: under skin, around kidneys, heart, in breasts and abdomen.

What it does: stores energy, insulates against heat loss, cushions organs.

Reticular 

Where: liver, spleen, lymph nodes, bone marrow.

What it does: forms a mesh framework that supports soft organs — like scaffolding.

Dense regular CT 

Where: tendons and ligaments.

What it does: very strong in one direction. Parallel fibres act like a rope — perfect for connecting muscle to bone or bone to bone.

Dense irregular CT 

Where: dermis of skin, joint capsules.

What it does: resists tearing from any direction because fibres run every which way.

Hyaline cartilage 

Where: joint surfaces, trachea rings, ribs, embryonic skeleton.

What it does: smooth surface reduces joint friction. Slightly flexible so it absorbs compression. Avascular so heals slowly.

Fibrocartilage 

Where: intervertebral discs, knee menisci, pubic symphysis.

What it does: toughest cartilage. Acts as a shock absorber between bones.

Elastic cartilage 

Where: outer ear, epiglottis.

What it does: holds shape but can bend and spring back. More flexible than hyaline.

Bone 

Where: skeleton.

What it does: supports the body, protects organs, stores minerals, produces blood cells in bone marrow. Cells: osteoblasts build it, osteocytes maintain it, osteoclasts break it down.

Blood 

Where: blood vessels everywhere.

What it does: transports oxygen, nutrients, hormones, waste and immune cells around the body. Note: it is connective tissue because it has cells floating in a liquid matrix called plasma.

 Identify tissues as connective based on their characteristics (e.g. Connective
tissue has fibres)

Identify tissues as connective based on their characteristics

How to identify connective tissue

If you can tick these boxes, it is connective tissue.

  • Has an extracellular matrix The defining feature. There is non-living material surrounding the cells — this can be liquid (blood), gel-like (cartilage) or solid (bone).

  • Contains fibres Most connective tissues contain one or more of these fibres sitting in the matrix:

  • Collagen fibres — strong, resist stretching

  • Elastic fibres — can stretch and spring back

  • Reticular fibres — fine mesh-like network

  • Cells are scattered Cells sit spread out within the matrix. They are NOT tightly packed together like epithelial cells.
  • Always found internally Connective tissue is never exposed to the outside world. It always sits inside the body.
  • Comes from mesenchyme All connective tissue originates from the same embryonic tissue called mesenchyme.
  • Variable vascularity Some types are richly supplied with blood vessels (bone, areolar). Some have none at all (cartilage, which is why it heals so slowly).

 Describe the different membranes found within the human body (e.g. Serous

Different membranes within the body

Body membranes

Serous membrane Where: lines closed body cavities — pleura (lungs), pericardium (heart), peritoneum (abdomen). What it does: produces watery fluid so organs can slide against each other without friction.

Mucous membrane Where: lines cavities open to the outside — airways, gut, urinary tract, reproductive tract. What it does: produces mucus to trap pathogens and particles. Also helps with absorption and secretion.

Cutaneous membrane Where: entire outer surface of the body — this is just the skin. What it does: tough waterproof barrier against damage, pathogens and water loss. The only dry membrane.

Synovial membrane Where: inside moveable joints — knee, hip, shoulder. What it does: produces synovial fluid to lubricate joints and deliver nutrients to cartilage inside.

Quick comparison

Membrane Location Function
Serous Closed cavities — lungs, heart, abdomen Produces watery fluid to reduce friction
Mucous Open cavities — airways, gut, urinary, reproductive Secretes mucus, traps pathogens
Cutaneous Outer body surface (skin) Waterproof protective barrier
Synovial Inside joint cavities Produces fluid to lubricate joints

 Describe the structure and function of neurons and neuroglia

Structure and function of neurons and neuroglia

Neurons and Neuroglia – Structure and Function

1. Neurons (Nerve Cells)

Structure of a Neuron

Neurons are specialized cells designed to transmit information. They have three main parts:

  • Dendrites
    • Short, branching extensions from the cell body
    • Function: receive incoming signals from other neurons or receptors
  • Cell body (Soma)
    • Contains the nucleus and most organelles
    • Function: integrates incoming signals and maintains cell functions
  • Axon
    • A long projection that carries signals away from the cell body
    • Can be very long (e.g., from spinal cord to muscles)
    • Ends in axon terminals, which release neurotransmitters to communicate with other cells
  • Myelin sheath
    • A fatty insulating layer around some axons
    • Produced by certain neuroglia
    • Function: increases the speed of nerve impulse transmission
  • Nodes of Ranvier
    • Gaps between myelin segments
    • Allow faster signal conduction by letting impulses "jump" between nodes

Function of Neurons

Neurons are responsible for communication within the nervous system through electrical and chemical signals.

Steps of signal transmission:

  1. Dendrites receive a stimulus
  2. The cell body processes the information
  3. An electrical impulse travels along the axon
  4. At the axon terminal, neurotransmitters are released across a synapse to another cell

Types of neurons (briefly):

  • Sensory neurons: carry information from receptors to the central nervous system
  • Motor neurons: carry signals from the central nervous system to muscles or glands
  • Interneurons: connect neurons within the brain and spinal cord

2. Neuroglia (Glial Cells)

What are Neuroglia?

Neuroglia are non-excitable cells that support, protect, and maintain neurons. They are more numerous than neurons and are essential for proper nervous system function.

Types and Functions of Neuroglia

  • Astrocytes
    • Provide structural support
    • Help regulate the chemical environment
    • Contribute to the blood-brain barrier
  • Oligodendrocytes (central nervous system)
    • Form the myelin sheath around axons
  • Schwann cells (peripheral nervous system)
    • Also form myelin sheath
    • Assist in nerve repair
  • Microglia
    • Act as immune cells
    • Remove waste, pathogens, and damaged cells
  • Ependymal cells
    • Line cavities in the brain and spinal cord
    • Help produce and circulate cerebrospinal fluid

Functions of Neuroglia (Summary)

  • Support and protect neurons
  • Supply nutrients and oxygen
  • Form myelin sheath for insulation
  • Maintain homeostasis (stable internal environment)
  • Defend against infection and remove debris

Key Differences

Feature Neurons Neuroglia
Function Transmit nerve impulses Support and maintain neurons
Electrical activity Yes (excitable) No (non-excitable)
Structure Dendrites, soma, axon No long axon
Number Fewer More numerous
Ability to divide Limited Can divide

Simple Summary

  • Neurons are specialized cells that transmit electrical signals for communication.
  • Neuroglia are supporting cells that protect, nourish, and assist neurons in functioning efficiently.

 Describe the stages of tissue repair (e.g. Inflammation, granulation etc.)

Stages of tissue repair

Stages of Tissue Repair

Tissue repair is the process the body uses to fix damaged tissue. It can result in:

  • Regeneration – replacement with the same type of tissue (normal function restored)
  • Fibrosis – replacement with scar tissue (does not function the same)

1. Haemostasis

What happens:

  • Occurs immediately after injury
  • Blood vessels constrict to reduce bleeding
  • A blood clot forms

Purpose:

  • Stops blood loss
  • Forms a temporary barrier to protect the wound

2. Inflammation

What happens:

  • The body responds to injury
  • Blood vessels dilate (become wider)
  • White blood cells move to the area

Signs:

  • Redness
  • Heat
  • Swelling
  • Pain

Purpose:

  • Prevents infection
  • Removes dead cells and pathogens
  • Prepares the area for healing

3. Organisation (Granulation Stage)

What happens:

  • Granulation tissue forms (new soft tissue)
  • New capillaries (blood vessels) grow
  • Fibroblasts produce collagen (connective tissue fibres)

Purpose:

  • Restores blood supply
  • Begins rebuilding the tissue structure

4. Regeneration / Fibrosis (Final Repair)

Two possible outcomes:

Regeneration

  • Tissue is replaced with same type of cells
  • Normal function returns

Fibrosis

  • Tissue is replaced with scar tissue (fibrous connective tissue)
  • Function is reduced or lost

Purpose:

  • Completes the healing process
  • Provides permanent repair

Simple Summary (Easy to Remember)

  1. Haemostasis – stops bleeding
  2. Inflammation – cleans and protects
  3. Organisation (granulation) – rebuilds tissue and blood supply
  4. Regeneration/Fibrosis – final repair (normal tissue or scar)

 Explain the roles and uses of micro-organisms (e.g. Making antibiotics)

Roles and Uses of Micro-organisms

Roles and Uses of Micro-organisms

Micro-organisms are tiny living things that are found everywhere: in soil, water, air, and on and inside our bodies.

Why they are important:

  • They break down dead plants and animals, recycling nutrients back into the environment.
  • Some bacteria help plants grow by fixing nitrogen in soil.
  • Many live on our bodies as normal flora, protecting us from harmful microbes.
  • Humans use micro-organisms to make foods (bread, cheese, yoghurt, beer, wine).
  • Some micro-organisms are used to make medicines, especially antibiotics, such as penicillin made by the fungus Penicillium.

Structural Features of Bacteria

 Explain how and why some bacteria form endospores

Endospores

Endospores

Some bacteria protect themselves by forming endospores.

  • Endospores form when conditions are harsh (heat, lack of food, chemicals).
  • The bacterium dehydrates, seals its DNA in a tough coat, and becomes inactive.
  • Endospores are very resistant and can survive for years.
  • When conditions improve, the spore becomes an active bacterium again.
  • Example: Clostridium species.

 Explain what a nosocomial/HCAI infection is and common causative agents

Nosocomial / HCAI Infections & Their Common Causative Agents

Nosocomial / HCAI Infections

  • A nosocomial or healthcare‑associated infection (HCAI) is an infection acquired during healthcare treatment that was not present when the patient was admitted.
  • These infections commonly occur due to invasive procedures, contaminated equipment, or poor hand hygiene in healthcare settings.

Common Causative Agents

  • Staphylococcus aureus (including MRSA) causes wound, skin, and bloodstream infections.
  • Escherichia coli commonly causes urinary tract infections, especially in catheterised patients.
  • Clostridioides difficile causes diarrhoea, particularly after antibiotic use.
  • Pseudomonas and Klebsiella species cause pneumonia and bloodstream infections in vulnerable patients.

 Explain factors that contribute to the occurrence of HCAI

Factors That Contribute to the Occurrence of HCAI

Factors That Contribute to the Occurrence of HCAI

  • Poor hand hygiene allows micro‑organisms to spread between patients.
  • Invasive procedures such as surgery, catheters, and IV lines create entry points for pathogens.
  • Contaminated equipment or surfaces increase the risk of infection.
  • Overcrowding and vulnerable patients with weakened immunity increase transmission risk.
  • Inappropriate antibiotic use encourages the development of resistant organisms.

 List the components of the chain of infection and explain how the chain of
infection can be broken at different links

Chain of Infection & How to break it

Chain of Infection

  1. Germ (Infectious Agent)
    → The thing that causes disease
  2. Where it lives (Reservoir)
    → Where the germ stays (people, animals, surfaces)
  3. Way out (Portal of Exit)
    → How it leaves (coughing, blood, fluids)
  4. How it spreads (Transmission)
    → How it travels (touch, air, droplets)
  5. Way in (Portal of Entry)
    → How it enters the body (mouth, nose, cuts)
  6. Person at risk (Susceptible Host)
    → Someone who can get sick

How to Break the Chain

  1. Kill the germ → Clean, disinfect, use medicine
  2. Clean the area → Keep surroundings safe
  3. Block the exit → Cover coughs, wear masks
  4. Stop the spread → Wash hands, use PPE
  5. Block entry → Protect wounds, use gloves
  6. Protect people → Vaccines, healthy lifestyle

 Explain standard precautions and additional precautions

Standard and Additional Precautions in Healthcare Settings

Standard and Additional Precautions

Standard Precautions

  • Hand hygiene is performed before and after patient contact to prevent the spread of micro‑organisms.
  • Gloves and gowns are worn to protect both the patient and healthcare worker from contact with body fluids.
  • Safe waste handling is used to prevent exposure to infectious materials and contamination of the environment.

Additional Precautions

  • Contact precautions are used when infections spread through direct contact or contaminated surfaces, such as MRSA.
  • Droplet precautions are used for infections spread by coughing or sneezing, such as influenza.
  • Airborne precautions are used for infections spread through the air over long distances, such as tuberculosis or measles.

 Explain what antiseptics are

Antiseptics explained

Antiseptics

  • Antiseptics are chemicals used on living tissue such as skin or wounds to reduce or kill micro‑organisms.
  • They help prevent infection and sepsis during wound care and medical procedures.
  • Antiseptics reduce microbial numbers but do not completely sterilise tissue.
  • They differ from disinfectants, which are used on non‑living surfaces.

 Explain how antibacterial drugs kill bacteria

How Antibacterial Drugs Work

How Antibacterial Drugs Work

Antibacterial Drugs will:

  • Damages the cell walls of bacteria
  • Stop protein production since bacteria needs proteins to grow
  • Disrupt metabolism which can starve bacteria
  • Damages membranes DNA or contents, which prevent proliferation and survival
